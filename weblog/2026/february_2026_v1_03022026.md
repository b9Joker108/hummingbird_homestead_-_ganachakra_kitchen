**Can you find me market dominant surfactants in Australia?**

Market-dominant surfactants in Australia can be thought of in two layers: (1) dominant **chemistries**, and (2) dominant **local producers/suppliers**.

**Dominant surfactant chemistries**

For volume, the Australian market looks similar to other developed markets: synthetic anionics for detergency dominate, with growing but still smaller bio-based segments. [1][2][3]

- **Synthetic anionic surfactants (largest by volume/revenue)**  
  - Linear alkylbenzene sulfonates (LAS/LABSA) for household laundry and hard-surface cleaners. [2][3][4]
  - Other anionics (e.g. secondary alkane sulfonates, alpha-olefin sulfonates) as co-surfactants and in some industrial cleaners. [1][3]

- **Other major classes present at scale**  
  - Alcohol ethoxylates and related nonionics for detergents, personal care and industrial cleaning (often paired with LAS). [1][3]
  - Amphoterics (e.g. betaines) and mild anionics for shampoos and body wash in the fast-growing personal care segment. [1][3]
  - Specialized sulfosuccinate wetting agents for mining and industrial processing. [5]

- **Bio-based/green surfactants (fast-growing, not yet dominant by tonnage)**  
  - Sugar-based, plant-oil-derived and other bio-based surfactants used in “green” home care and personal care, with rapid growth but smaller base compared to synthetics. [1][3][6][7]

**Dominant producers and suppliers in Australia**

If by “market dominant” you mean who actually makes/controls supply domestically rather than the global majors’ finished FMCG brands, a few companies stand out. [1][8][3][5]

- **Albright & Wilson (A&W)** – Sydney  
  - Described as *“the leading manufacturer of surfactants and supplier of phosphates in the Australian market”* with a comprehensive range covering anionic, cationic, amphoteric and specialty blends for home and personal care. [8]
  - Long-established local producer (manufacturing since 1978), so effectively one of the key backbone suppliers into detergents/personal care. [8]

- **Tri‑Tech Chemical Company – Melbourne**  
  - Identified as *“Australia’s leading producer of sulfosuccinate surfactants”*, supplying wetting agents for mineral dewatering and industrial uses, plus oleochemical esters and emulsifiers used in cleaners, coatings, water treatment and other industrial processes. [5]
  - Important if you care about dominant surfactants in mining, metal processing and certain industrial cleaners. [5]

- **Segment view rather than named brands**  
  - Household soaps and detergents are the largest application segment for surfactants in Australia by revenue, which in practice means high-tonnage LAS plus nonionics. [3]
  - Synthetic surfactants as a substrate class hold the largest market share, with bio-based products the fastest-growing but still secondary in absolute size. [3][6][7]

**How to use this for sourcing**

Depending on whether you’re interested in lab-scale, formulation, or bulk sourcing:

**I just had a Chinese 'cabanossi' sweetmeat. A preserved meat sausage and savoury. Traditional?**

It sounds like you ate something very real and traditional in Chinese cuisine — but it wouldn’t be called “cabanossi.” What you had was almost certainly a form of Chinese preserved sausage (臘腸 lap cheong), which is indeed traditional, though quite different from European cabanossi.

What You Likely Ate
Chinese preserved sausages fall under the broader category of 臘味 (lap mei), a family of cured, dried, often sweet–savory meats used across southern China. These include:

- 臘腸 (lap cheong) — sweet, aromatic pork sausages  
- 膶腸 (yun cheong) — liver sausages  
- 臘肉 (lap yuk) — cured pork belly  
- 臘鴨 (lap ap) — cured duck

They are typically:

- Air‑dried and cured, not smoked like cabanossi  
- Sweet–savory, often with sugar, soy sauce, rice wine  
- Firm and waxy, sliced thin or steamed before eating  
- Used in rice dishes, stir‑fries, claypot rice, and New Year foods

This aligns with your description of a sweetmeat, preserved, sausage, and savoury.

Why It Reminded You of Cabanossi
Cabanossi is a European smoked, dried pork/beef sausage with a chewy snap. Chinese preserved sausages share:

- A dense, cured texture  
- A ready-to-eat or lightly cooked profile  
- A strong, distinctive seasoning

But the flavour profile is different: Chinese versions are sweeter, wine‑fragrant, and not smoked.

Is It Traditional?
Yes — extremely.  
Chinese preserved sausages have been made for centuries, especially in Guangdong, Fujian, and Hong Kong. They are considered:

- Winter preservation foods  
- Festive foods, especially around Lunar New Year  
- Staples in Cantonese cooking

Modern producers (e.g., Wing Wah in Hong Kong) still follow traditional methods: marinating pork with rose wine and soy sauce, stuffing into natural casings, and air‑drying. 

Why You Might Have Encountered a “Cabanossi‑like” Version
In Australia, many Chinese butchers and grocers produce hybrid or locally adapted versions:

- Slightly smokier  
- Firmer or drier  
- Less sweet  
- More snack‑like  

These can feel closer to cabanossi, especially if eaten plain rather than cooked into a dish.

Quick Comparison Table

| Feature | Chinese Lap Cheong | European Cabanossi |
|--------|--------------------|--------------------|
| Preservation | Air‑dried, cured | Smoked + dried |
| Flavour | Sweet, wine‑aromatic | Savoury, smoky |
| Texture | Firm, waxy | Chewy, snappy |
| Use | Cooked into dishes | Eaten as snack |
| Tradition | Southern Chinese | Central/Eastern European |

**Sand, coconut oil, peppermint oil, sugar and what ... should I mix to make my soles babycheek smoov?**

For baby‑cheek smooth soles, you want:  
1) **a *gentle* scrub**, and  
2) **a rich [occlusive](https://en.wiktionary.org/w/index.php?title=occlusive&oldid=88326793) moisturiser** afterward.  

Given your ingredients (sand, coconut oil, peppermint oil, sugar), here’s how to make it work *safely* and what else to add.

***

## 1. Don’t use sand on skin

Skip the **sand** entirely: it’s too sharp and irregular, can micro‑tear skin and drive grit into cracks. Use **sugar** as your main exfoliant instead; it dissolves and self‑limits abrasion.

***

## 2. Simple peppermint foot scrub

Use this in the shower or over a basin; 1–2× per week is enough.

**Ingredients**

- 2 tbsp white sugar (finer = gentler)  
- 1 tbsp coconut oil (melted but cool)  
- 2–4 drops peppermint essential oil  
- Optional: 1 tsp fine salt (if your soles are really tough)  

**Method**

- Mix sugar and coconut oil to a wet sand texture: you want it **thick**, not runny.  
- Add peppermint oil and stir well.  
- On damp feet, massage into soles and heels for 1–3 minutes, focusing on rough spots.  
- Rinse off thoroughly and pat dry.

If your heels are cracked or sensitive, drop the salt and keep pressure light.

***

## 3. What to mix in for real “baby‑cheek” softness

After exfoliation, you need something richer than just coconut oil.

Add **one of these** to your kit:

- **Shea butter**: mix 1–2 parts shea butter with 1 part coconut oil to make a thick balm.  
- **Beeswax + oil**: melt 1 part beeswax with 3–4 parts oil (coconut + another liquid oil like olive or jojoba); when it cools, it becomes a salve.  

**Example night balm**

- 2 tbsp shea butter  
- 1 tbsp coconut oil  
- 2–3 drops peppermint oil  

Gently melt, stir, cool until thick and creamy, then massage a small amount into clean, dry feet at night and put on thin cotton socks.

***

## 4. Routine that actually changes the skin

- 1–2× per week: peppermint sugar scrub in the shower.  
- Every night for 1–2 weeks: apply your balm generously + cotton socks.  
- Once soles are soft, drop to maintenance (balm 2–3× per week).

If you already have deep cracks, keep scrub pressure very light and prioritise balm + socks; the smoothness will come from consistent moisturising, not more abrasion.

**Now, what strips vegetable, fruit & nut oils from a bath: the executive homemade mixture pls?**

Your executive homemade tub oil stripper: **vinegar + dish soap + baking soda**. This is the gold standard for emulsifying and lifting vegetable/fruit/nut oils (coconut, jojoba, almond, etc.) without harsh chemicals. [1]

## The precise mixture

**Spray cleaner (emulsifies oils)**  
- 3 cups hot water  
- 1 cup white vinegar (cuts oil films)  
- 1 tbsp liquid dish soap (surfactant; emulsifies oils into water)  

**Abrasive booster**  
- 2–4 tbsp baking soda (absorbs oil, mild scrub)  

## Exact application

1. **Sprinkle** baking soda liberally over the oily ring, tub floor, and walls. Let sit 5 min to absorb. [1]
2. **Mix spray**: combine water, vinegar, dish soap in a spray bottle; shake well. [1]
3. **Spray heavily** over baking soda; let fizz 2–3 min (vinegar activates soda). [1]
4. **Scrub** with nylon scrub pad or soft brush in circular motions until oil lifts (1–2 min per section).  
5. **Rinse** thoroughly with hot water; dry with microfiber or towel.  

**Yield**: enough for 2–3 full tubs. Scale as needed. [1]

## Why it works

- **Vinegar**: acetic acid dissolves oil bonds.  
- **Dish soap**: micelles grab oil molecules and rinse them away.  
- **Baking soda**: soaks up residue + provides traction for scrubbing.  

This handles coconut oil (your foot balm base) and any nut/fruit oils perfectly. Do it right after draining while oils are fresh. [1]
