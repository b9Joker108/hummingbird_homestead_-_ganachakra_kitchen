**In English, what is the earliest attested Svenska term(s)/analogues for "January" (prior to Christo-Latinisation) according to native and indogene traditions for the most expansive historical region known as Sverige or even of this region/territory prior to this nomenclature?**

***Short answer:*** The oldest indigenous Swedish names for “January” are forms like torsmånad / tormånad (Old Swedish thorsmanadher, tormonet), attested in medieval and early modern sources and preserved in regional dialects; these names likely pre‑date Latinized januari in spoken tradition though the earliest surviving written attestations are later.

**Evidence and earliest attestations**

***Earliest written attestations:*** Modern lexica record torsmånad (Old Swedish thorsmanadher) and related spellings; some sources date a written attestation to the early 15th century, while folkloric and dialectal evidence points to forms used already in Old Norse contexts. In printed almanacs of the 18th century the old Swedish names were explicitly printed alongside Latin names, showing continuity of the indigenous terms into the early modern period.

**Forms and variants**

- Common historical forms: thorsmanadher, torsmånad, tormånad, sometimes rendered torremånad in older orthography.  
- Related Old Norse term: Some traditions link the month to Old Norse lexical items such as Þorri or to mythic/folk names; the exact relationship is debated in scholarship.

**Geographic and dialectal distribution**

***Regional survival:*** The indigenous names survive in dialects across southern and central Sweden — e.g., Skåne, Blekinge, Småland, Närke, Värmland and Uppland — where people historically referred to January as torsmånad or tormånad in folk calendars and oral tradition.

**Origins and interpretations**

There are two main interpretive strands in the literature:  
- ***Mythological association:*** A folk etymology connects tors- to the god Thor, producing an interpretation like “Thor’s month,” though many scholars treat this as a later folk reinterpretation rather than the original sense.  
- ***Descriptive origin:*** Linguists and folklorists often prefer a derivation from an adjective meaning “dry” (Old Swedish torr / torre), so tormånad would originally mean something like “the dry month,” describing winter conditions rather than invoking a deity.

**Historical usage in calendars**

Printed almanacs from the 18th century show Latin month names (e.g., Januarius) printed together with the older Swedish names, indicating that the indigenous terms were still recognized and used in popular contexts even after Latinization of official nomenclature.

**Perplexity, is Thorsmanadher contiguous with January?**

No. **Þorsmánuðr / Þorri** in the Old Norse–Icelandic calendar roughly corresponds to mid‑January to mid‑February and so only partially overlaps with January, not the whole month. [1][2]

**Name and meaning**

- Old Swedish **thorsmanadher** (modern Swedish **torsmånad**) literally means “Thor’s month,” from *Tor* (Thor) + *månad* (month). [3]
- In Icelandic the cognate month is **Þorri**, the fourth winter month of the traditional calendar. [1][2]

**Position in the year**

- In reconstructions of the Old Norse/Icelandic calendar, **Þorri** begins around **mid‑January** and runs to **mid‑February**, starting on a specific weekday (e.g. Friday between 19–26 January) rather than on 1 January. [4][1][2]
- Modern Icelandic explanations usually gloss Þorri simply as **“January/February”**, reflecting that it straddles those Gregorian months rather than coinciding exactly with either. [5][2]

**Relation to January**

- Because Þorri starts **after** the beginning of January and continues into February, it is **not contiguous with January as a whole**; instead, January is split between the preceding month (e.g. Mörsugur/Yule-month) and Þorri. [1][6]
