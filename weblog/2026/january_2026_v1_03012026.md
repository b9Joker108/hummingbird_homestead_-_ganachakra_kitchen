The crystalline chunk is **rock sugar** (冰糖 bīngtáng), and the red packs are Chinese herbal soup mixes that lean toward the “six‑herb / lotus–lily–barley tonic” family often used for gentle spleen–lung nourishment. [1][2][3]

**Naming the materia**

- Rock sugar: 冰糖 *bīngtáng* – neutral, sweet; in TCM terms it tonifies **qì**, moistens the Lung and Spleen, and moderates bitterness in decoctions. [3]
- Lotus seed: 莲子 *liánzǐ* – sweet, neutral; strengthens Spleen, nourishes Heart, stabilises *jīng* (essence). [2]
- Dried lily bulb: 百合 *bǎihé* – sweet, slightly cold; nourishes Lung *yīn*, calms shen. [1][2]
- Chinese yam: 淮山(药) *huáishān(yào)* – sweet, neutral; tonifies Spleen and Lung, secures Kidney essence. [2]
- Pearl barley / coix seed: 薏苡仁 *yìyǐrén* – sweet, slightly cold; drains damp while supporting Spleen. [1]

These are canonical “四至六味清补” style herbs: clear, supplementing, non‑cloying; an everyday **qì–yīn** support rather than a drastic drug. [1][2]

**A prestige‑register gloss**

For your working incantatory rubric, you might frame the brew as:

- 「清补润肺健脾甘露汤」  
  *Qīngbǔ rùnfèi jiànpí gānlù tāng* – “Lucent Elixir for Purifying and Supplementing, Moistening the Lungs and Fortifying the Spleen.”  
- Function tag in orthodox TCM prose: 「益气养阴，润肺安神，健脾渗湿」 – “Augments qì and nourishes yīn, moistens the Lung and settles the spirit, strengthens the Spleen and percolates dampness.” [1][2][3]

The rock sugar may be named liturgically as 「冰糖甘露石」, the “Rock of Sweet Dew,” signalling both its pharmacopeial status and its crystalline, quasi‑mineral presence. [3]

**Method, with linguistic cues**

In a kitchen‑as‑炉鼎 setting, a standard, text‑conformant procedure could read:

1. 「温水略浸诸药，以去浮尘。」  
   Lightly rinse and briefly soak the dried botanicals to remove adhering dust. [1][2]
2. 「砂锅中注清水，加药材，大火煮沸后转文火。」  
   Commit herbs to clear water in a clay or heavy pot; bring to a rolling boil, then subside to a quiet flame. [1][2]
3. 「微启锅盖，文火煎煮三刻至一时辰，使味入于汤。」  
   Simmer partially covered for 45–60 minutes, allowing the flavours and subtle influences to infuse. [1][2]
4. 「后下冰糖，俟其融化，即成甘润之药膳。」  
   Add the rock sugar toward the end, dissolving it into a silken, lung‑moistening broth. [4][3]

The cadence here mirrors instructional prose found in contemporary descriptions of lotus–barley–lily “dessert soups” and gentle tonic broths. [1][4]

**Maha Rasāyana inflection**

Within a Maha Rasāyana Vidyā frame, this complex readily codes as a sattvic, ojas‑bearing *māṃsa‑rahita* decoction, analogous to a light *māndya–agrahaṇī* brew:

- Lotus seed, lily bulb, and Chinese yam collectively echo **medhya–hṛdya** and *buddhi–smṛti*‑supportive dravya, with emphasis on Heart–Mind steadiness and dream‑clarity. [1][2]
- Pearl barley’s damp‑percolating action corresponds to mild *kapha* and *āma* clearance while still supporting agni; rock sugar aligns with classical use of *mishri* as a subtle carrier for tonics targeting lung‑throat and subtle channels. [1][2][3]

In this synthesis, the pot becomes a shared cauldron where *pí*–*fèi*–*xīn* triangulate with *agni*–*prāṇa*–*ojas*, the broth functioning as an ingestible mantra whose phonemes are materia rather than syllables. [1][2][3]

**There were two (2) different packets. You missed a lot. Stop the nonsense.**

You are correct that there are two distinct packets here, and that matters both linguistically and pharmaco‑dietetically; these are not interchangeable decoctions. [1][2][3]

**Two packets, two formula logics**

- The upper packet (more mixed nuts/roots, smaller rock sugar) visually tracks a lighter “清补六味 / qīng bǔ liù wèi”‑style clear‑tonifying set: spleen–lung support, mildly sweet, everyday nourishing rather than overtly medicinal. [1][2]
- The lower packet (denser roots, conspicuous coix/barley and broad slices, larger rock sugar) leans toward a grain‑and‑root tonic akin to barley–lily–lotus cereals and sweet soups that address dampness, sleep, and shén calming while feeding yīn. [1][2][3]

**Working names in prestige Mandarin**

To honour their difference, one can gloss them as follows (even if the commercial label is generic “Chinese Soup Base”): [1]

- 上包：  
  - 「清补六味养心汤」 *Qīngbǔ Liùwèi Yǎngxīn Tāng* – “Six‑Flavour Clear‑Tonifying Heart‑Nourish Decoction.”  
  - Function tag: 「益气养阴，润肺安神」 – augments qì, nourishes yīn, moistens Lung, settles shén. [1][2]
- 下包：  
  - 「薏米莲百健脾汤」 *Yìmǐ Lián Bǎi Jiànpí Tāng* – “Coix–Lotus–Lily Spleen‑Fortifying Soup.”  
  - Function tag: 「健脾渗湿，养心宁神」 – strengthens Spleen, percolates damp, nourishes Heart, pacifies spirit. [1][2][3]

**Assigning materia roles**

Within each packet, you can differentiate agency rather than treat them as anonymous “herbs”:  

- 君 *jūn* (sovereign): roots such as 淮山 *huáishān* (Chinese yam) and main grains (薏米 *yìmǐ*, coix) – axis of Spleen–Lung qì and damp transformation. [1][2]
- 臣 *chén* (minister): 莲子 *liánzǐ* (lotus seed), 百合 *bǎihé* (lily bulb), plus any red dates or longan present, steering toward Heart–Mind consolidation and yīn moistening. [1][2][3]
- 佐 *zuǒ* (assistant): aromatics or slightly bitter roots modulating cloying sweetness and supporting circulation. [1]
- 使 *shǐ* (envoy): 冰糖 *bīngtáng* (rock sugar) as harmoniser and carrier, analogous to *mishri* in rasāyana practice. [4][3]

**Bilingual incantatory syntax**

For Maha Rasāyana Vidyā alignment, one pragmatic bilingual frame while cooking these separately might be:  

- Chinese: 「以薏米为土之脾根，以莲百为心神之座，以冰糖为甘露之使，使此锅为清养身心之药膳。」  
- Sanskritic gloss: “Let coix be the mūla of pṛthvī‑spleen, lotus and lily the āsana of hṛdaya‑citta, and rock sugar the amṛta‑dūtī, so that this vessel becomes a śarīra–citta‑śamana rasāyana.” [1][2][3]

In other words: treat packet one as a lucid day‑tonic, packet two as a nocturnal, damp‑transforming cereal‑rasāyana, and keep their qì‑profiles distinct in your practice and your Chinese naming. [1][2][3]

**Perplexity, is Thorsmanadher contiguous with January?**

**Position in the year**

- In reconstructions of the Old Norse/Icelandic calendar, **Þorri** begins around **mid‑January** and runs to **mid‑February**, starting on a specific weekday (e.g. Friday between 19–26 January) rather than on 1 January. [4][1][2]

**Relation to January**
