**Please tell me all about the Skandi historicity and spatiotemporality and traditional preparatory process of dried cod?**

Dried cod, known as stockfish or *t√∏rrfisk* in Scandinavia, originated as a vital preservation method for cod (*Gadus morhua*), especially the migratory *skrei* variety, dating back to the Stone Age in northern Norway's Lofoten Islands.[1][2] This practice enabled survival in harsh northern climates and fueled Viking-era trade, with references in *Egil‚Äôs Saga* showing exports to Europe over 1,000 years ago.[3][4] By the Middle Ages, Bergen emerged as a Hanseatic hub, exporting thousands of tons annually to Catholic regions for Lent observance, marking stockfish as Norway's oldest sustained export.[3][5]

## Spatiotemporal Aspects
Production centers on Lofoten and Vester√•len in northern Norway, where *skrei* migrates from the Barents Sea to spawn from January to April, aligning with peak fishing (*lofotfiske*).[2][6] The spatiotemporal pattern exploits cold winds (around 0¬∞C), light breezes, and low humidity from February to June for optimal drying on wooden racks (*hjell*), preventing freezing or spoilage.[6][7] Historical trade radiated from these Arctic sites southward to Bergen, then Europe, with genomic evidence tracing 600-year cod movements from Lofoten to medieval Oslo and beyond.[8]

## Traditional Preparation
Fresh *skrei* heads inland from December, caught during spawning; fish are gutted, decapitated, and paired (head-to-tail or tail-to-tail) with string through the eyes.[6][9] They hang on outdoor racks for 2-4 months in Lofoten's climate, naturally air-drying to 30-50% moisture without salt or additives, developing a firm, fermented texture via cold-adapted bacteria.[7][9] Post-drying (May-June), stockfish grades by quality (e.g., *superior* from Hanseatic terms) and ships globally; rehydration involves 6-7 days soaking in cold water, changed twice daily.[10][3]

**Life, training, and institutional location**

Bose was born in 1858 in Mymensingh (now in Bangladesh), educated in Calcutta, and then at Cambridge and the University of London, where he initially attempted medical studies before switching to physics under Lord Rayleigh.[1][7] Returning to India in 1885, he joined Presidency College, Calcutta, as professor of physics (1885‚Äì1915), where he taught under conditions of institutional racism, at one point refusing his salary for three years until paid on parity with his British colleagues.[2][8] In 1917 he founded the Bose Research Institute (Bose Institute) in Calcutta‚ÄîAsia‚Äôs first explicitly interdisciplinary research centre‚Äîwhich he directed until his death and which became a major node for physics, biophysics, and plant molecular biology.[1][2]

**Physics: microwaves, semiconductors, priority**

From 1894 onwards Bose designed and built an entire experimental infrastructure for millimetre‚Äëwave research, including resonant cavities, dielectric lenses, and waveguides, at a time when the electromagnetic spectrum beyond the centimetre range was technologically intractable.[8][7] In 1895 he publicly demonstrated wireless transmission of electromagnetic waves in Calcutta, ringing a bell and detonating gunpowder at a distance‚Äîan experiment that, in priority terms, places him at least contemporaneous with, and in some respects ahead of, Marconi‚Äôs early radio work.[8][7] Crucially, he was the first to use what would now be recognised as a semiconductor junction detector for radio waves, anticipating solid‚Äëstate rectification devices that underpin twentieth‚Äëcentury electronics, though he declined to aggressively patent or commercialise his apparatus.[1][8]

**Plant physiology and ‚Äúplant neurobiology‚Äù**

After retirement from Presidency College, Bose pivoted almost entirely to plant physiology, investigating excitation, conduction, and ‚Äúfatigue‚Äù in plant tissues with a level of quantitative electrophysiological rigour unusual for botany at the time.[3][2] Using exquisitely sensitive instruments of his own design‚Äînotably the crescograph and torsional recorders‚Äîhe measured growth movements, turgor changes, and electrical responses in plants to stimuli such as light, temperature, chemicals, and mechanical injury, arguing that both plants and animals share a common electrophysiological basis for irritability.[3][1] Recent historians and plant biologists have retrospectively positioned him as a ‚Äúfather of plant neurobiology,‚Äù and scholarship on the 1920s American backlash against his ideas has foregrounded how scientific objections were entangled with racialised dismissal of a colonised, non‚ÄëEuropean experimenter.[3][4]

**Epistemic hybridity, Vedanta, and anticolonial science**

Bose‚Äôs discourse on science is neither a simple replication of metropolitan positivism nor an uncomplicated invocation of ‚Äúancient wisdom,‚Äù but an attempt to braid modern laboratory practice with Vedantic ontologies of continuity between organic and inorganic.[6][3] In critical reconstruction, his insistence on a shared continuum of responsiveness in metals, plants, and animals has been read as both a metaphysical claim (resonant with Advaitic monism) and a strategic anticolonial move against Western dualisms that had placed colonised subjects, nature, and the ‚Äúvegetative‚Äù on the same rung of a civilisational hierarchy.[6][4] Scholars of religion and science in South Asia have used Bose as a key case in the ‚Äúnationalisation‚Äù of science: he reassures Bengali publics that experimental physics and physiology are not alien to Indic traditions while also insisting that scientific authority can be locally produced, not merely imported.[6][7]

**Literary production and science fiction**

In the Bengali literary sphere, Bose is now widely considered the father of Bengali science fiction, with stories such as ‚ÄúRunaway Cyclone‚Äù (1896; revised 1921) mobilising scientific and meteorological motifs to stage anti‚Äëimperial critiques.[5][1] Recent scholarship reads ‚ÄúRunaway Cyclone‚Äù as an early experiment in combining science fiction with magical realist textures, in which colonial technologies of surveillance and control are symbolically undone by forces of nature that elude imperial prediction, thereby inverting the epistemic asymmetry central to late‚ÄëVictorian technoscience.[5] This literary work feeds back into his scientific persona, elaborating a philosophy of science that is simultaneously cosmopolitan in technique and resistant to the racialised universalism of Western expertise.[5][6]

During his lifetime Bose received significant recognition‚Äîknighthood in 1917 and election as the first Indian scientist fellow of the Royal Society in 1920‚Äîyet in Euro‚ÄëAmerican historiography of radio and electrophysiology his contributions were long marginalised.[2][8] Historians of science now emphasise how colonial prejudices, intellectual property regimes, and Bose‚Äôs own distaste for commercialisation contributed to this partial erasure, even as his institute continued to make notable contributions to cosmic‚Äëray physics, microbial pathogenesis, and plant molecular biology.[2][9] The current wave of reassessment in plant neurobiology, postcolonial STS, and South Asian intellectual history frames Bose as a nodal figure for thinking the politics of experiment, the metaphysics of ‚Äúsensitivity,‚Äù and the conditions under which colonised scientists could both inhabit and reconfigure global scientific modernity.[3][4][6]

## Greek Suffixes Denoting Creation

Suffixes like -poiesis, -poie, -peia, -poeia, -poesis, and adjectival forms such as -poeic, -poetic, -poietic, -poietical derive from the Greek root *poiein*, meaning "to make" or "to create." [2][6] In medical terminology, they specifically indicate the "creation," "production," or "formation" of the entity named by the preceding lexical root. [2][6][8]

## Medical Usage and Examples

These suffixes form nouns describing physiological processes, especially blood cell production (e.g., *hematopoiesis* for blood formation). [6] They combine with roots via a vowel (often "o") to create compound terms, such as *lymphopoiesis* (lymph formation). [3][6] Adjectival variants like -poietic describe related qualities, as in *mythopoetic* for myth-creating. [4]

## Etymological and Terminological Context

Originating from Ancient Greek *poiƒìsis* ("a making"), these elements entered medical lexicon through Neo-Latin, adhering to rules where roots link to suffixes without mixing Greek and Latin inconsistently. [2][6][7] Examples include *pharmacopoeia* (medicine-making) and *onomatopoeia* (name-making by sound imitation). [4] This system ensures precise, standardized nomenclature in anatomy and pathology.
