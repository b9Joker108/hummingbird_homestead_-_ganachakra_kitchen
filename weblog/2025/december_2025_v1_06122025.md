The International Society for Krishna Consciousness (ISKCON), founded in 1966 by A.C. Bhaktivedanta Swami Prabhupada in New York as a nonprofit religious organization, represents a modern continuation of the Gaudiya Vaishnava tradition within Hinduism. Its governance emphasizes spiritual purity, global unity, and adherence to Prabhupada's teachings, with the Governing Body Commission (GBC) as the pinnacle of authority since its establishment in 1970. This structure has evolved through reforms, addressing challenges like leadership crises and legal issues, to support over 800 temples, centers, and affiliated projects worldwide.

**Historical Foundations and Purpose**

Prabhupada envisioned ISKCON as a vehicle for propagating Krishna consciousness through chanting, book distribution, temple establishment, and simple living. The "Constitution of Association" from 1966 outlines core objectives: disseminating spiritual knowledge, uniting members with Krishna, promoting congregational chanting, erecting holy places, and publishing literature. Post-1977, following Prabhupada's passing, the GBC assumed full managerial control, guided by his will and directives like the Direction of Management. An ongoing constitution draft, under review as of March 2025, seeks to codify these into a unified document, incorporating amendments for registration and cooperation.

**Governing Bodies and Hierarchical Structure**

The GBC, comprising 32 members (primarily zonal secretaries elected internally), convenes annually in Mayapur for parliamentary-style decision-making via majority vote. It oversees zones, approves gurus, sets standards, and enforces discipline. Supporting the GBC is an Executive Committee (EC) with rotating chairman, vice chairman, and secretary roles to manage emergencies and implement resolutions. Regional Governing Bodies (RGBs) handle area-specific issues in places like Europe or India, while an Executive Director, aided by divisional directors in areas like legal and devotee care, executes global initiatives.

Ministers specialize in fields such as education, communications, and youth, advising leaders and reporting annually. GBC Deputies, elected for three-year terms, review proposals and assist tasks. Locally, temple presidents manage operations under zonal supervision, focusing on worship, finances, and community. Standing committees address philosophy, sannyasa, child protection, and more.

This hierarchy ensures unity while allowing regional adaptation, with all roles under GBC authority.

Disputes escalate from local presidents to zonal secretaries, then GBC, with measures like censure, probation, suspension, or expulsion for offenses such as schism or abuse. ## Greek Suffixes Denoting Creation

Suffixes like -poiesis, -poie, -peia, -poeia, -poesis, and adjectival forms such as -poeic, -poetic, -poietic, -poietical derive from the Greek root *poiein*, meaning "to make" or "to create." [2][6] In medical terminology, they specifically indicate the "creation," "production," or "formation" of the entity named by the preceding lexical root. [2][6][8]

## Medical Usage and Examples

These suffixes form nouns describing physiological processes, especially blood cell production (e.g., *hematopoiesis* for blood formation). [6] They combine with roots via a vowel (often "o") to create compound terms, such as *lymphopoiesis* (lymph formation). [3][6] Adjectival variants like -poietic describe related qualities, as in *mythopoetic* for myth-creating. [4]

## Etymological and Terminological Context

Originating from Ancient Greek *poiƒìsis* ("a making"), these elements entered medical lexicon through Neo-Latin, adhering to rules where roots link to suffixes without mixing Greek and Latin inconsistently. [2][6][7] Examples include *pharmacopoeia* (medicine-making) and *onomatopoeia* (name-making by sound imitation). [4] This system ensures precise, standardized nomenclature in anatomy and pathology.
