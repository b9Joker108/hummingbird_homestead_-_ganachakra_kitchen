The "second chamber" you mention in the Australian House of Representatives is indeed real and is called the **Federation Chamber**. It is an auxiliary or secondary chamber of the lower house, distinct from the Senate which is the upper house of the Parliament. This Federation Chamber functions as a supplementary forum within the House of Representatives itself, not a separate house of Parliament.

**Origin**
The Federation Chamber originated in 1994 as the "Main Committee," created by the House Standing Committee on Procedure to alleviate the increasing legislative workload[1]. The idea was to provide a second debating arena to enable more detailed discussions on bills and other matters without extending the overall hours members spend in the Parliament.

**Purpose and Function**
The Federation Chamber's purpose is to support the House of Representatives by serving as a venue for:
- Additional debates on legislation, beyond what the main chamber can accommodate.
- Scrutiny of bills and government business.
- Private members' business and constituency matters.
- Facilitating a wider range of parliamentary activities including statements, grievances, and budget debates (specifically appropriation bills with broad discussion scopes on public affairs)[1].

**Responsibilities and Purview**
- It deals with referred bills and business by decision of the House.
- Decisions here are provisional and reported back to the main House for final resolution, particularly as the Federation Chamber cannot resolve dissent by vote but only by voice; unresolved matters revert to the full House.
- The Federation Chamber cannot be the venue for matters that determine government confidence or the formation/dissolution of the government; these take place in the main chamber[1].
- It helps "bridge" the workload pressure, effectively functioning as a "pressure valve" that allows more work of the House to be done without increasing sitting hours excessively[1].

**Operational Characteristics**
- The Federation Chamber meets almost every sitting day.
- It is subordinate to the main House chamber and ceases operation during full House divisions to allow members to attend votes in the main chamber.
- It increasingly resembles a miniature version of the main House in scope but without the power to settle debates fully or create government mandates[1].

**Summary**
The Federation Chamber is a procedural innovation and an internal secondary debating chamber of the Australian House of Representatives. It originated to relieve legislative pressure, broaden opportunities for parliamentary participation, and enable more detailed consideration of legislation and other matters without extending the day-to-day hours of the House or risking government stability decisions. It supplements, but does not supplant, the constitutional role of the single lower house as prescribed in the Parliament of Australia Act and Constitution[1].

This institution is unique in Westminster-derived parliaments, providing a flexible deliberative forum within the lower house itself, sometimes described informally as the House's "second chamber" or "Chamber of Federation" for its role in facilitating federal legislative work[1].

The Federation Chamber is indeed the second chamber within the Australian House of Representatives, and it operates as an alternative debating venue to the main chamber of the House. 

**Origin and Purpose**
- Established in 1994 initially as the "Main Committee" and renamed the Federation Chamber in 2012, this chamber was created to manage the increasing parliamentary workload and allow additional debate space while the main chamber focuses on key business[1][2].
- Its creation aimed to allow two streams of parliamentary business concurrently, relieving pressure on the main chamber.

**Function and Responsibilities**
- It primarily debates the second reading and consideration in detail stages of bills, committee and delegation business, private members' business, and documents presented to the House[1][2].
- Business referred to the Federation Chamber must later be confirmed by the main House as it is a subordinate body.
- Unlike the main chamber, it does not conduct divisions (recorded votes); decisions are made "on the voices," and unresolved questions are reported back to the House for resolution[1][2].
- It facilitates more inclusive participation by allowing private members to raise matters, present petitions, and speak in constituency statements, adjournment debates, and grievance debates[1].

**Operational Aspects**
- The Federation Chamber meets almost every sitting day in a dedicated committee room set as a miniature version of the main chamber with seating for 38 members and public and media galleries[1][2].
- The Deputy Speaker chairs the chamber and controls procedure and order, enforcing rules similar to those in the main chamber but without the power to cast deciding votes[2].
- The chamber operates with cooperation in mind, with mechanisms designed to minimize disorder and ensure smooth proceedings[1][2].

**Summary**
The Federation Chamber is an internal and subordinate second chamber within the Australian House of Representatives. It allows additional legislative debate and business, easing the workload of the main chamber, while still operating under its authority. It has a defined procedural role with specific limitations (no divisions, decisions require House confirmation) and is essential for efficient parliamentary function and inclusive member participation.

This is a unique innovation in the Westminster parliamentary system, sometimes informally called a “second chamber” of the lower house or “Chamber of Federation” because it provides a distinct forum within the House of Representatives for detailed consideration of federal legislation and business[1][2].
