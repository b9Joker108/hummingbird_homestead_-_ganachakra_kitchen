What was the Dreaming called in indigenous tongues?

The Dreaming, a central spiritual and cosmological concept among Australian Aboriginal peoples, is known by many different names across the richly diverse Indigenous languages of Australia. It is a multi-faceted worldview encompassing creation, ancestral beings, law, and ongoing spiritual connection to the land and cosmos.

### Indigenous Names for the Dreaming

1. **Alcheringa / Altjira / Altjirrinja (Arrernte/Aranda language, Central Australia)**  
   - One of the earliest recorded terms by anthropologists Spencer and Gillen, often translated as "Everywhen" or eternal, uncreated reality[1][2][3].

2. **Jukurrpa / Tjukurrpa (Warlpiri and Western Desert languages)**  
   - Signifies not just creation stories but also law, morality, and life patterns that persist eternally as guiding principles for Aboriginal people of the Central and Western deserts[1][2][4][5].

3. **Manguny (Martu Wangka, Western Desert and Pilbara regions)**  
   - Refers to the sacred time and the ancestral creative stories specific to Martu peoples[3].

4. **Wongar (North-East Arnhem Land languages)**  
   - Used by some Aboriginal groups in Arnhem Land to describe the creation and ancestral spiritual time[1][3].

5. **Ngarrangkarni / Ngarranggarni (Kija and other East Kimberley languages)**  
   - A term expressing the sacred creative time and law linked to ancestral beings within Kimberley cultures[3].

6. **Ungud / Wungud (Ngarinyin people, North-Western Australia)**  
   - Spiritual concept related to the Dreaming, often associated with ancestral serpent beings and the creative processes[3].

7. **Nura (Dharug language, Sydney Basin)**  
   - Name used to refer to "country" and Dreaming concept integrating place, ancestry, and spiritual law[1].

8. **Nyitting (Noongar language, South-West Australia)**  
   - Closely linked to creation stories and spiritual law in the south-western Aboriginal communities[1].

9. Other region-specific names exist due to the linguistic diversity with over 250 languages and hundreds of dialects historically[3].

### Conceptual Note
- "Dreaming" or "Dreamtime" as an English term emerged from anthropological discourse and is **not a direct or absolute translation** of any one Indigenous word but a catch-all term for a complex, multi-layered spiritual reality[1][6].
- Many Aboriginal people emphasize that the Dreaming is a **living reality rather than a past "time"**, reflected linguistically in terms that convey ongoing spiritual presence, law, and ancestral guidance[1][2][6].
- The terms collectively convey a sacred, eternal, and cyclical worldview underpinning Aboriginal connection to land, identity, and community.
