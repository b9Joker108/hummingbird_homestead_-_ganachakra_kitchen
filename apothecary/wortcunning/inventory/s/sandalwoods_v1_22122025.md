# Sandalwoods: A Survey #

Many types of wood are used medicinally and prepared in different ways for healing and mystical and energetic purposes for Homo sapiens saliens in the indigenous medicine-ways of phytomedicinal traditions Glocally, I am presently interested in ALL the Sandalwoods?

All the main sandalwoods belong to the genus **Santalum**, but different species have distinct medicinal, ritual, and energetic profiles across cultures.[1][2]

## Major Santalum species

- **Santalum album**  
  - Indian / white sandalwood; heartwood and oil are classic in Ayurveda, Tibetan, and Chinese medicine for cooling, anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and anxiolytic actions, especially for skin, urinary, hepatic, and febrile conditions.[3][4][5]
  - Energetically framed as **cooling, sattvic, and mind-clearing**, used to pacify excess heat, anger, and agitation, and to deepen meditation and mantra recitation.[6][4][7]

- **Santalum spicatum**  
  - Australian sandalwood, native to southern and western Australia; traditionally used by First Nations peoples and now in aromatherapy for skin issues, sleep, anxiety, and as a grounding, centering fragrance.[1][8][9]
  - Often described as **drier, smokier, more astringent** than S. album; metaphysically used for boundary-setting, subtle “earth” stabilization, and dream/sleep work.[8]

- **Santalum austrocaledonicum & allies**  
  - New Caledonian / Pacific sandalwoods (with several varieties) contributing to the modern “Pacific sandalwood” trade.[1][2]
  - Oils typically sit between Indian and Australian in scent; used in perfumery and ritual for **harmonizing, heart-softening, and relational** work, with milder cooling/soothing medicinal attributions in local traditions.[2][8]

- **Hawaiian sandalwoods (S. paniculatum, S. freycinetianum, S. ellipticum, S. haleakalae, S. pyrularium)**  
  - Known as **ʻiliahi**, historically significant in Kanaka Maoli healing and ceremony; overharvested in the 19th century.[1]
  - Contemporary ethnobotanical framing emphasizes **breath, chest, and spirit**: clearing grief and chest tightness, grounding identity, and reconnecting with ʻāina (land).[2][8]

- **Other Santalum spp. (e.g., S. acuminatum, S. murrayanum, S. lanceolatum, S. yasi, S. insulare, S. macgregorii, S. obtusifolium, S. boninense, etc.)**  
  - Some (like **S. acuminatum**, desert quandong) are more valued for edible fruits, while others contribute regionally to incense, local medicine, or mixed “sandalwood” oils.[1][2]
  - Their **energetic signatures** in practice tend to be more local and lineage-specific, often combining food, medicine, and ancestor/land veneration.[1][2]

## Shared medicinal motifs across sandalwoods

- **Cooling & anti-inflammatory:** Used for heat-related skin eruptions, inflamed mucosa, “hot” urinary conditions, fevers, and irritability.[3][4][5][7]
- **Antimicrobial & vulnerary:** Applied to infections and wounds (especially in S. album) and as an adjunct in respiratory and digestive complaints.[3][4][5]
- **Neuropsychological effects:** Inhalation or anointing is associated with reduced anxiety, improved sleep, and deepened meditative focus.[3][6][8][7]

## Mystical and energetic uses

- **Axis mundi / altar wood:** Carved into malas, murtis, tsa-tsas, and ritual implements; seen as a **vessel for mantra and deity presence**, particularly for cool, luminous, moon-like deities and guru lineages.[6][4][7]
- **Purificatory smoke:** Incense and chip offerings are used to **clear heavy or agitated atmospheres**, invite refined presences, and establish ritual purity before other operations.[6][4][7]
- **Subtle body regulation:** In traditional Chinese and Tibetan framings, sandalwood helps regulate **Qi / rlung**, unbinding chest and heart constrictions and harmonizing breath and spirit.[4][5][7]

## Working glocally with “all the sandalwoods”

- For **Ayurveda/Vedic and bhakti** praxis: prioritize ethically sourced **S. album**, reserving it for head, heart, and altar work where the cooling, sattvic quality is central. [3][6][4]
- For **Australian land-based magic and medicine**: **S. spicatum** is ideal, especially when integrated with local Country protocols and First Nations knowledge where possible.[8][9]
- For **experimental comparative work**: track perceptual and ritual differences between Indian, Australian, and Pacific sandalwood oils/woods in a praxis journal—note shifts in breath, mood, imagery, and dream-content session by session.[6][8][7]
